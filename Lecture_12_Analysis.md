## Page 2: Managers and Management
- **Organization Definition**: A deliberate arrangement of people brought together to accomplish some specific purpose.
- **Key Question**: Who are managers and what do they do?

## Page 3: Characteristics of Organizations
1.  **Goals**: Distinct purpose expressed as a goal.
2.  **People**: Essential for making decisions and activities.
3.  **Structure**: Defines behavior of members.
- **Hierarchy**:
    - **Top Managers**: Make decisions about direction, policies.
    - **Middle Managers**: Translate goals set by top managers.
    - **First-Line Managers**: Direct activities of nonmanagerial employees.
    - **Nonmanagerial Employees**: Work directly on tasks.

## Page 4: Business Model Canvas (Standard Framework)
- **Infrastructure (Left)**:
    - **Key Partners**: Suppliers, Supply chain.
    - **Key Activities**: Necessary to deliver Value Prop.
    - **Key Resources**: Assets required.
- **Offering (Center)**:
    - **Value Propositions**: Core value provided to customer. What problem is solved?
- **Customers (Right)**:
    - **Customer Relationships**: Type of relationship (Personal, Automated).
    - **Channels**: How to reach customer.
    - **Customer Segments**: Who are we creating value for?
- **Finance (Bottom)**:
    - **Cost Structure**: Major costs (Fixed/Variable).
    - **Revenue Streams**: How money is generated (Asset sale, subscription, etc.).

## Page 5: What is Management?
- **Definition**: Process of getting things done **Effectively** and **Efficiently**, with and through other people.
- **Performance Management Cycle (PDCA)**:
    1.  **Plan**: Set objectives, develop plan.
    2.  **Do**: Implement, build capabilities.
    3.  **Check**: Track progress, identify problems.
    4.  **Act**: Solve problems, share learnings.
- **Efficiency vs. Effectiveness**:
    - **Efficiency (Means)**: Resource Usage. **Low Waste**.
    - **Effectiveness (Ends)**: Goal Attainment. **High Attainment**.

## Page 6: What do Managers Do?
- **Four Functions**:
    1.  **Planning**: Defining goals, strategy.
    2.  **Organizing**: Determining tasks, grouping, reporting lines.
    3.  **Leading**: Motivating, directing, resolving conflicts.
    4.  **Controlling**: Monitoring performance, correcting deviations.
- **Time Distribution by Level**:
    - **First-Level**: High **Leading** (51%).
    - **Middle**: Balanced (Leading 36%, Organizing 33%).
    - **Top**: High **Organizing** (36%) & **Planning** (28%).
- **Matrix**: Managers use resources (Human, Financial, Physical, Info) and carry out functions to achieve goals.

## Page 7: Roles and Skills
- **Mintzberg's Managerial Roles** (Examples):
    - **Interpersonal**: Figurehead, Leader, Liaison.
    - **Informational**: Monitor, Disseminator, Spokesperson.
    - **Decisional**: Entrepreneur, Disturbance Handler, Resource Allocator, Negotiator.
- **Skills**:
    - **Interpersonal Skills**: Working with people.
    - **Conceptual Skills**: Analyze complex situations.
    - **Technical Skills**: Job-specific knowledge.
    - **Political Skills**: Build power base, connections.

## Page 8: Evolution of Management Thought (Timeline)
- **Classical Approaches (1911-1947)**
- **Behavioral Approach (Late 1700s-1950s)**
- **Quantitative Approach (1940s-1950s)**
- **Contemporary Approaches (1960s-present)**

## Pages 9-11: Classical Approaches
1.  **Scientific Management (1911)**:
    - **Frederick Taylor** ("Father of Scientific Management").
    - Goal: Determine the "**One Best Way**" to increase productivity.
    - **"Time and Motion Studies"**: Job specialization, Standardization, Financial incentives.
2.  **Administrative Theory (1916)**:
    - **Henri Fayol**. Focus on Organizational Practices.
    - **5 Functions**: Plan, Organize, Command, Coordinate, Control.
    - **14 Principles** (e.g., Division of Work, Authority, Unity of Command, Scalar Chain, Esprit de Corps).
3.  **Bureaucracy Theory (1921)**:
    - **Max Weber**.
    - **Bureaucracy**: Organizational structure characterized by rules, standardized processes, division of labor, hierarchy, and impersonal interactions.

## Page 12: Behavioral Approach
- Focus: Actions of workers, motivation, leading.
- **Hawthorne Studies (1924-Mid 1930s)**:
    - **Insights**: Individual and **Group behavior** affect performance.
- **Human Relations Movement (1930s-1950s)**:
    - Belief: "A **satisfied** worker was believed to be a **productive** worker."
    - Suggestions: Employee participation, praise, being nice.
- **Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs** (Chart shown):
    - Physiological -> Safety -> Love/Belonging -> Esteem -> Self-actualization.

## Page 13: Quantitative Approach
- **Period**: 1940s-1950s (Evolved from WWII military solutions).
- **Focus**: Application of statistics, optimization models, simulations.
- **Total Quality Management (TQM)**:
    - **Deming** and **Juran**.
    - Philosophy devoted to **continual improvement** and responding to customer needs.

## Page 14: Contemporary Approaches (1960s-Present)
- **Organization as an Open System**:
    - **Inputs** (Materials, HR, Capital, Tech) -> **Transformation Process** (Work activities) -> **Outputs** (Products, Financial Results).
    - **Feedback** loops. Interaction with **Environment**.
- **Contingency Approach**:
    - "No single way to manage people or work is best in every situation."
    - **IF... THEN** logic.
    - Managers must study individual and **situational differences**.
